Data Integrity

FDA Data Integrity Training Online — ALCOA, the 18 Questions and What Inspectors Find

GMPify Training·June 26, 2026·7 min read·Sources: FDA Data Integrity Guidance December 2018 | 21 CFR 211 | FDA Warning Letter database | fda.gov

Data integrity is the second most frequently cited area in FDA drug warning letters. The December 2018 FDA Guidance for Industry on Data Integrity and Compliance With Drug CGMP — its Questions and Answers document covering eighteen specific regulatory interpretations — remains the definitive statement of FDA's data integrity expectations for pharmaceutical manufacturers. Understanding it in depth is essential for QA, QC and laboratory professionals at any site that generates, reviews or stores CGMP data.

This guide explains what FDA's data integrity requirements actually are, where manufacturers most commonly fall short, and what effective online data integrity training should cover.

What FDA's data integrity requirements actually say

FDA does not have a standalone data integrity regulation. Data integrity requirements are embedded throughout 21 CFR Parts 210, 211 and 212 — the CGMP regulations for finished pharmaceuticals. The December 2018 guidance clarifies how these existing regulations apply to the full range of data integrity challenges in modern pharmaceutical manufacturing.

The five ALCOA principles — Attributable, Legible, Contemporaneous, Original and Accurate — describe the characteristics that complete, consistent and accurate data must have. They derive from specific CFR sections. Attributable comes from 211.188(b)(11) and 211.194(a)(7)(8). Contemporaneous comes from 211.100(b). Original comes from 211.180 and 211.194. Accurate is the broadest principle, cutting across virtually every data recording requirement in Parts 210 and 211.

What makes the FDA guidance particularly important is not the ALCOA definition, which most pharmaceutical professionals know, but the eighteen specific questions it addresses — questions about shared logins, invalidation of test results, audit trail review, chromatographic data retention, FDA inspection access and the management responsibilities that underpin an effective data integrity programme.

The five data integrity areas that generate the most warning letter citations

Shared login credentials (Q5). When personnel share system login credentials, a unique individual cannot be identified as the person who performed an action. This violates the attributability requirement of 21 CFR 211.68(b), 211.188(b)(11) and 211.194(a)(7)(8) regardless of whether the system maintains an audit trail. The audit trail records what was done — it cannot record who did it if multiple people use the same credentials.

Testing into compliance (Q2). Running a test until you get a passing result and discarding the failing attempts is one of the most serious data integrity violations FDA encounters. Q2 of the guidance is explicit: all results, including out-of-specification results, must be retained. An assignable cause must be documented before an OOS result can be invalidated. There is no circumstance in which a failing result can simply be discarded.

Chromatographic data retention (Q14). All processing attempts for chromatographic data must be retained — not just the final accepted result. Every integration attempt, every reprocessing attempt, the original raw data and the final result must all be available for inspection with documented justification for any reprocessing. Deleting intermediate results is a data integrity violation regardless of whether the final result was correct.

Audit trail review (Q7). Audit trails must be reviewed as part of routine record review — at the same frequency as the CGMP data itself. A site that enables audit trails but never reviews them has not met the requirement. The QCU is responsible for reviewing audit trails for evidence of data integrity issues including deleted records, modified results and backdated entries.

Management responsibility (Q18). Management with executive responsibility must create a quality culture where data integrity is an organisational core value. FDA has been explicit that systemic data integrity problems are management failures, not individual failures. Warning letters consistently cite inadequate management oversight as a contributing factor in data integrity cases.

The most dangerous data integrity gap

Many sites have data integrity SOPs but no genuine quality culture around data integrity. The SOP describes what to do. Without management engagement, an environment where personnel feel safe raising data integrity concerns and adequate resourcing for audit trail review, the SOP exists on paper and nowhere else.

What effective online data integrity training covers

Data integrity training that genuinely prepares pharmaceutical professionals for inspections and real-world quality decisions should go beyond a definition of ALCOA. It should address all eighteen questions in the FDA guidance, covering the specific regulatory citations for each, the most common compliance failures associated with each question and the management and system design principles that support sustainable data integrity.

It should also address the real-world context of modern pharmaceutical laboratories — where electronic records are generated automatically, where software is increasingly cloud-based, where chromatography data systems have complex audit trail capabilities and where the intersection between data integrity and computer software assurance creates compliance obligations that span multiple regulatory frameworks.
