Investigation and CAPA

Why Human Error is Never an Acceptable Root Cause Under 21 CFR 211.192

GMPify Training·June 22, 2026·6 min read ·Sources: 21 CFR 211.192 | FDA Warning Letter database | GMP Navigator 2024 | ECA Academy 2024 | Pharmaceutical Online 2025 | Certainty Software 2026 | RAPS FY2024

Human error accounts for approximately 50% of all quality incidents and related problems in pharmaceutical manufacturing. It is the most cited root cause in deviation investigations. It is also the most frequently cited inadequate root cause in FDA warning letters.

This is not a coincidence. It is a pattern that FDA has been documenting, citing and escalating for years — and that shows no sign of improving. 21 CFR 211.192 was cited 116 times in FY2024 alone, second only to 211.22(d) in the most common inspection observations. The majority of those citations share a common thread: an investigation that stopped at the symptom rather than identifying the systemic cause.

FDA warning letter language — a direct citation

"Inadequate investigations can lead to unidentified root causes, ineffective CAPA, and recurring problems that compromise the ability to manufacture safe and effective drug products."

What 21 CFR 211.192 actually requires

21 CFR 211.192 states that any unexplained discrepancy or the failure of a batch or any of its components to meet any of its specifications shall be thoroughly investigated, whether or not the batch has already been distributed. The investigation must extend to other batches of the same drug product and other drug products that may have been associated with the specific failure or discrepancy.

Three words in that requirement carry the most regulatory weight: thoroughly investigated. FDA has consistently interpreted "thorough" to mean an investigation that identifies the root cause of the deviation — the underlying systemic condition that allowed the failure to occur — not merely the immediate event that triggered it.

Human error identifies the immediate event. It does not identify the underlying condition. It is therefore not a thorough investigation — and FDA says so explicitly in warning letter after warning letter.

Why "human error" fails as a root cause — the logic

Consider a deviation where an operator adds the wrong component to a batch. The investigation concludes: human error. Retraining conducted. CAPA closed.

This investigation has answered one question: who made the mistake and what mistake was made. It has not answered the more important questions:

The chain of whys that "human error" never reaches

1
The operator added the wrong component. (Event identified — this is what "human error" captures)
↓ Why?
2
Because the two components were stored adjacent to each other with similar labelling.
↓ Why?
3
Because the storage layout was not reviewed when a new component was introduced six months ago.
↓ Why?
4
Because the change control process does not include a storage layout review step for new material introductions.
↓ Root cause
5
The change control SOP does not require a storage proximity review when new materials are introduced. This is the systemic root cause. Retraining the operator does not fix it.

The difference between stopping at step 1 and reaching step 5 is the difference between a 211.192 observation and a compliant investigation. The CAPA that flows from step 5 — updating the change control SOP — will prevent the next operator from making the same error in the same conditions. The CAPA that flows from step 1 — retraining — will not.

What FDA inspectors are finding

FDA warning letters from 2024 and 2025 consistently describe the same investigation failure patterns. A sample of the language FDA uses when citing 211.192 inadequacies:

"You concluded your investigations without a root cause determination."

FDA Warning Letter pattern — GMP Navigator analysis 2024

"Your response is inadequate because you did not address how you will ensure that investigations contain adequate root cause determinations, corrective action and preventive action, and effectiveness checks."

Bell International Laboratories Warning Letter, February 2024

"Your firm's quality unit failed to extend product quality complaint investigations to other batches or APIs potentially associated with the root cause, failure, or deviation."

Sichuan Deebio Pharmaceutical Warning Letter, February 2024

The third citation is particularly important. 211.192 requires investigations to extend to other potentially affected batches. An investigation that concludes "human error" by definition cannot extend the scope meaningfully — because if the root cause is systemic, other batches manufactured under the same systemic condition are potentially affected and need to be assessed.

The retraining trap

The most common CAPA for a human error root cause is retraining. Industry analysis consistently shows that the vast majority of investigations concluding human error have identified a symptom — not a cause — and prescribed a CAPA that does not address the actual condition that allowed the error to occur.

FDA's position on retraining as a CAPA is well documented. Retraining is an appropriate CAPA only when the investigation has demonstrated that the root cause was genuinely a knowledge or competency gap — that the operator did not know the correct procedure or was not competent to execute it. In the majority of cases where human error is cited, the operator did know the correct procedure. They simply made an error in conditions that were designed, through facility layout, labelling design, procedure complexity or workflow pressure, to make that error easy to make.

Addressing the conditions that make errors easy is the systemic root cause. Retraining the person who made the error in those conditions is not.

What an adequate investigation looks like

An investigation that satisfies the 211.192 "thoroughly investigated" standard will typically include all of the following:

  • A factual description of the event — what happened, when, who was involved, what product and batch was affected
  • An immediate impact assessment — what other batches or products may be affected by this deviation
  • A root cause analysis using a structured methodology — five-why analysis, Ishikawa fishbone, fault tree analysis or equivalent — that traces the event back to its systemic cause
  • A determination of whether the root cause is confirmed or probable — with supporting evidence for the conclusion
  • A CAPA that addresses the systemic root cause — not the event itself
  • An effectiveness check with defined criteria — how will the site verify that the CAPA actually prevented recurrence?
  • Extension to other potentially affected batches — documented assessment of scope

The recurring problem — and its cost

The reason 211.192 has appeared in the top four FDA inspection observations for four consecutive years is not that pharmaceutical companies lack investigation procedures. It is that investigations that conclude "human error" do not fix the conditions that caused the deviation. The same deviation recurs. The next investigation concludes human error again. The pattern repeats.

Research cited in FDA warning letter analysis found that firms with weak 483 responses — which typically include inadequate root cause investigations — face a greater than 50% probability of escalation to warning letter. More than half of firms that received drug warning letters in FY2023 and FY2024 had already received 483 findings and failed to resolve them at the systemic level.

The cost of not identifying the real root cause is not just regulatory. It is the cost of the next batch failure, the next investigation, the next CAPA, the next inspection observation, and potentially the next warning letter — all of which could have been prevented by one thorough investigation the first time.

The bottom line

Human error is an event descriptor — it identifies what happened but not why the system allowed it to happen. Under 21 CFR 211.192, a thorough investigation is one that identifies the underlying systemic condition that made the error possible. That condition is the root cause. Addressing it is the CAPA. Retraining the individual is not a CAPA — it is a response to a symptom. FDA has made this position clear in hundreds of warning letters over many years. The citations keep coming because investigations keep stopping too soon.
